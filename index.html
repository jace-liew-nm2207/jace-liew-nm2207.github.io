What is deforestation, and why should I care?

Click on Koko the koala!

Deforestation is the conversion of forested areas to non-forest land use such as arable land, urban use, logged area or wasteland.
According to the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO), deforestation is also the long-term reduction of tree canopy cover below the 10% threshold.

As nations develop and economies grow, the thirst for resources has also increased in order to satisfy our current global population of 8 billion people. Deforestation is threfore occurring at a rapid rate and we are not sure how long more the earth can hang on to its last resources. Up to 15 billion trees are now being cut down every year across the world, species are going extinct and millions of lives are affected.

What does deforestation look like on a global scale?

Click on the play/pause buttons, or drag the slider to see the deforestation rate over the years!

Deforestation has consistently occurred globally over the years, especially in developing countries such as Brazil and Indonesia as land is needed for urbanisation and agriculture.

However, with the increasing pressure to be sustainable on many companies and governments, the rate of forest loss has declined over the past three decades. The annual rate of deforestation was estimated at 10 million hectares between 2015-2020, compared with 12 million during 2010-2015. The area of forest under protection has also reached roughly 726 million hectares, nearly 200 million more than in 1990.

Still, there is cause for great concern, as the current rate of 10 million hectares of forests lost a year is still extremely high. Furthermore, past deforestation acts have left permanent consequences on the environment.

But who is responsible for all these deforestation?

From the doughnut chart above, Brazil and the Americas is responsible for most of the deforestation happening. This data was from 2013, however, 10 years later, Brazil is still the top contributor to deforestation - deforestation in the Brazilian Amazon during the first half of 2022 broke all records, a result of the increasing destruction taking place under the presidency of Jair Bolsonaro.

South America also owns the largest remaining rainforest, the Amazon. Since 1978 about one million square kilometers of Amazon rainforest have been destroyed across Brazil, Peru, Colombia, Bolivia, Venezuela, Suriname, Guyana, and French Guiana. The Amazon contains nearly a third of all the tropical rainforests left on Earth. Despite covering only around 1% of the planet’s surface, the Amazon rainforest is home to 10% of all the wildlife species. This makes South America both the greatest owner and destructor of biodiversity.

How much biodiversity have we lost?

We have talked about biodiversity loss in the Amazon, how about on a global scale?

The Living Planet Index (LPI) is published biennially in WWF's Living Planet Report(LPR), a leading science-based publication on the state of the planet and associated challenges and solutions. The Living Planet Index (LPI) measures the state of the world's biological diversity based on population trends of vertebrate species from terrestrial, freshwater and marine habitats. The index represents 31,821 populations of 5,230 species.

The LPI plays an important role in monitoring progress towards the post-2020 goals and targets negotiated at COP15. As seen from this graph, the LPI has decreased drastically from a value of 1 in 1970 to a mere 0.308 in 2018, a 69% drop in species population numbers. A main contributor to this huge drop is the loss of habitats, caused by deforestation.

But...what causes deforestation?

What is causing such a high demand for the red meat?

Forests provide food and shelter for biodiversity. More than half the world's land-based plants and animals, and three-quarters of all birds, live in and around forest. As mentioned above, deforestation has already caused drastic adverse impacts on diversity, causing beautiful species such as the Spix Macaw to go extinct.

Trees absorb and store carbon dioxide. If forests are cleared, or even disturbed, they release carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases. Forest loss and damage is the cause of around 10% of global warming. This can cause a chain of impacts and cause the imbalance of ecosystems. It can also cause a vicious cycle where global warming results in more wildfires, which adds to deforestation, exacerbating global warming.

With wild habitats destroyed and human life ever expanding, the line between animal and human areas blurs, opening the door to zoonotic diseases. In 2014, for example, the Ebola virus killed over 11,000 people in West Africa after fruit bats transmitted the disease to a toddler who was playing near trees where bats were roosting.

Over 1.6 billion people, many among the rural poor, depend on forests for food or fuel, and 70 million people worldwide - including many Indigenous communities - call forests home. Forests provide us with oxygen, shelter, jobs, water, nourishment and fuel. With so many people dependent on forests, the fate of our forests may determine our own fate as well. Millions of people rely directly on forests as their home or for making a living.

Take action.

Many people simply don’t realise that the food we eat and the things we consume can a cause of deforestation. To reduce deforestation, we need everyone to know about the problem. Talk to the people around you, raise awareness about the issue and what we stand to lose.

Be aware of what you are consuming. Consume only what you need, reduce consumption of meat and grow your own food whenever possible.
